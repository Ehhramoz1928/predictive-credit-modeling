This working assignment focuses on identifying consumer credit risk and predicting loan defaults by executing an end-to-end applied statistics workflow on a customer dataset of 600 unique accounts. By shifting the perspective away from theoretical mathematics toward high-stakes financial problem-solving, the study evaluates two distinct modeling philosophies using binary logistic regression. The first model analyzes how a borrower's credit utilization and broad demographic proxies like education affect their risk profile, while the second model optimizes the predictive framework by replacing demographics with hard behavioral metrics, specifically localized asset classes and immediate delinquency history. Ultimately, the project successfully achieves all its primary objectives. Both models display elite predictive quality by passing the Hosmer-Lemeshow goodness-of-fit test and achieving exceptional Area Under the Curve statistics above 0.98. The behavioral and asset-backed framework emerges as the superior model, improving overall accuracy to 94.17% and boosting precision to 95.58%. This key outcome proves that tracking real estate equity and recent missed payments significantly reduces false alarms, allowing financial institutions to avoid accidentally turning away safe borrowers, maximize interest revenue, and safely protect long-term corporate liquidity.

This statistical investigation demonstrates that consumer credit exposure is a highly dynamic hazard whose final real-world impact depends heavily on a borrower's structural assets and behavioral payment habits. Beyond the primary performance metrics, a critical finding from the results is the revealing contrast between different physical asset layers under Wald's significance testing. While holding real estate equity provides an immense, statistically significant layer of protection that aggressively pulls a borrower's default probability down into a low-risk category, holding rapidly depreciating or lower-tier property like vehicle ownership alone is statistically insignificant and provides no reliable safety cushion compared to holding no assets at all. Furthermore, the operational results reveal a subtle classification behavior in the recommended behavioral framework. Because the optimized framework yields slightly more false negatives than false positives, the system demonstrates an internal operational tendency to prioritize preserving a high true positive capture rate while remaining slightly more lenient on approving qualified borderline candidates rather than over-rejecting them. Ultimately, by substituting abstract demographic proxies with direct physical asset layers and tracking short-term payment delinquency, a lending institution can lower its systemic error, capture toxic capital-destructive defaults, and minimize costly false alarms that would otherwise result in lost interest revenue and contracted market share.

At the end, the project bridged the gap between abstract mathematical outputs and actual corporate strategy. While data science often focuses on maximizing sensitivity to catch every default, the results proved that maximizing positive predictive precision is directly linked to institutional profitability. A "false positive" occurs when a creditworthy applicant is erroneously flagged as a default risk and denied a loan, which carries a steep economic opportunity cost in lost interest revenue and contracted market share. Refining the model to boost precision to 95.58% successfully minimizes these false alarms, proving that algorithmic filters can safely expand consumer approvals while simultaneously protecting long-term corporate liquidity.
